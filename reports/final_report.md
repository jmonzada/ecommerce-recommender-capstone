## Step 1: Problem Understanding & Framing

Olist is a Brazilian marketplace that connects small sellers to big storefronts. When I profiled the data, one number ended up shaping the whole project: 96.9% of Olist's 96,096 customers have placed exactly one order, ever. That's a brutal retention picture, and it means the business question isn't "how do we rank products a bit better" — it's whether a recommender can pull more customers into a second purchase at all, and whether it can widen what buyers see beyond the same bestsellers. There's a seller-side angle too. Olist's pitch to small merchants is exposure, so a recommender that only ever surfaces the popular stuff quietly breaks the marketplace's own value proposition.

Task-wise this is a recommendation problem, but I've deliberately decomposed it into two stages, because that's how production recommenders actually work and because each stage answers a different question. Stage one is candidate generation: given a customer, produce a shortlist of plausible products using item-item collaborative filtering, content similarity, matrix factorization, and a popularity baseline that I fully expect to be hard to beat on data this sparse. Stage two is a supervised ranker — a binary classifier scoring "will this (customer, product) pair convert as a delivered purchase" over engineered features. I considered framing the whole thing as plain classification or as customer clustering, but neither actually delivers a ranked product list, which is the deliverable a marketplace needs. The two-stage split also has a practical modelling payoff: the ranker is a feature-based model, which is where explainability tools like SHAP genuinely work.

For technical metrics I'm using HitRate and Recall at K = 10, 50 and 100, plus NDCG@10, MRR and catalogue coverage for candidate generation, evaluated with bootstrap confidence intervals because the hit rates on a 32,951-product catalogue will be small numbers and I don't want to declare a "winner" based on noise. The ranker gets ROC-AUC, PR-AUC and F1 — with the honest caveat, stated up front, that these are computed against sampled negatives, so they're relative comparisons between models rather than absolute performance claims. The decision-grade number is neither of those alone: it's the end-to-end table showing whether the full two-stage pipeline beats candidate generation on HitRate@10 and NDCG@10 for held-out purchases.

On business KPIs I want to be careful not to oversell. Personalisation uplift — repeat-purchase rate, average order value — only applies to the roughly 3% of customers with any history. For the other 97%, the realistic value story is first-purchase cross-sell and region-aware merchandising, and I've scoped the KPIs that way rather than pretending the model personalises for everyone. The third KPI is seller-side exposure equity: the share of recommendations going to long-tail products and smaller sellers, which I'll measure directly in the fairness audit.

## Step 2: Data Collection & Understanding

I'm using the Brazilian E-Commerce Public Dataset by Olist ([kaggle.com/datasets/olistbr/brazilian-ecommerce](https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/olistbr/brazilian-ecommerce), CC BY-NC-SA 4.0) — 99,441 real anonymised orders from 2016–2018 across nine relational tables: orders, order items, customers, products, sellers, reviews, payments, geolocation, and a Portuguese-to-English category translation. I picked it over synthetic or ratings-style datasets because it's genuinely transactional — real prices, real freight costs, real delivery dates, real 1–5 review scores — which gives the feature engineering something to work with. The trade-off I accepted going in: no customer demographics (which reshapes the fairness audit, more on that in Step 5) and extreme interaction sparsity.

The profiling notebook (`notebooks/01_data_overview.ipynb`) drove one decision I'd call the most important in the project: the evaluation window dates. Olist's monthly volume tells a clear story — a tiny pilot in late 2016 (329 orders, with November 2016 missing entirely), steady growth through 2017, a plateau around 6–7k orders/month in 2018, and then a cliff after August 2018 where volume collapses to 16 and then 4 orders. Everything outside 2017-01 to 2018-08 is unusable, so my three-window scheme — features from 2017-01 to 2018-02, ranker training labels from 2018-03 to 2018-05, holdout from 2018-06 to 2018-08 — is pinned to those dates in `configs/windows.yaml`, and every aggregate feature downstream is computed strictly inside the feature window.

![Monthly order volume with evaluation windows](figures/01_monthly_order_volume.png)

The tables themselves are in decent shape, but not spotless, and a few quirks matter. `order_items` has one row per physical unit, so its 112,650 rows collapse to 101,987 unique customer–product pairs — anything building an interaction matrix has to dedupe first. The reviews table caught me out: `review_id` looks like a primary key but isn't (814 duplicated values, and 547 orders carry more than one review), so I keep only the latest review per order. Missingness is mostly interpretable rather than alarming — 88% of reviews have no title and 59% no message because most customers simply don't write text, and the 3% of orders missing a delivery date are, unsurprisingly, largely the undelivered ones. Prices are heavy-tailed: the median item costs about R$75 while the p99 sits near R$890 and the max at R$6,735, which is why I'll winsorise price-derived features later rather than let a handful of luxury items stretch every scale. Review scores skew happy — 77.1% are 4 or 5 — and 97.0% of orders reached `delivered` status, so filtering to delivered orders costs almost nothing. Geographically the dataset is very lopsided: 68.7% of customers are in the Southeast and just 1.9% in the North, a fact that directly triggers the minimum-group-size rules I set for the fairness audit.
